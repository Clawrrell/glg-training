Section 1

Letter of Representation (LOR)

📄 What Is an LOR?

  • A formal notice to the insurance company that our firm now represents the client
  • Once received, all communication must go through us — not directly to the client
  • Legally puts the insurer on notice of our representation
  • Triggers the insurer's obligation to deal with us, not the client
  • Sent to BOTH the at-fault driver's insurer AND our client's own insurer (for UM/UIM)

✅ What the LOR Does

The LOR is our formal flag planted in the ground. It says: "This client has an attorney. Don't call them. Don't take a recorded statement. Deal with us." Once the LOR is received, any communication the insurer has with our client (without going through us) could be a serious ethics violation.

1
Send within 24-48 hours of signing
As soon as the client signs the fee agreement, the LOR should go out. Every day without an LOR is a day the insurer can contact the client directly.
2
Send to all relevant insurers
At-fault driver's insurer, our client's UM/UIM insurer, MedPay carrier if applicable. Don't miss any.
3
Confirm receipt
Follow up to confirm the insurer received and acknowledged the LOR. Document the confirmation in CasePeer with the date and who confirmed.
4
Keep a copy in the file
Upload the signed LOR to CasePeer. If the insurer later claims they didn't receive it, you have proof of sending.

🚨 What Happens Without an LOR

Insurance adjusters will aggressively contact clients before an LOR arrives — asking for recorded statements, offering quick lowball settlements, and trying to close the claim fast. This can seriously damage or destroy a case. The LOR is your first line of defense.

Section 2

Letter of Protection (LOP)

🏥 What Is an LOP?

  • A promise to a medical provider that they will be paid from the client's settlement
  • Allows clients to receive medical treatment without paying upfront
  • The provider treats the client on credit — trusting that GLG will pay them at settlement
  • Common with chiropractors, orthopedists, pain management doctors, imaging centers
  • The LOP is signed by both the law firm and the client

✅ Why LOPs Matter

Many of our clients can't afford to pay medical bills out of pocket while waiting for a settlement. LOPs allow them to get the treatment they need, which (1) helps their health, and (2) builds the medical record that drives case value. Without treatment, there's no case.

⚠️ The LOP Creates a Lien

When a provider treats under an LOP, they have a medical lien — a legal right to be paid from the settlement proceeds. At disbursement, the LOP provider gets paid first, before the client receives their portion. Always track every LOP provider and their outstanding balance in CasePeer.

📋 LOR vs. LOP — Quick Reference

  • LOR = sent to the insurance company — "We represent this client"
  • LOP = sent to the medical provider — "Treat now, we'll pay you at settlement"
  • Different recipients, different purposes — don't mix them up
  • Both are early, critical steps in every case

🚨 Lien Reduction at Settlement

LOP providers often charge higher rates than insurance-negotiated rates. At settlement, we negotiate with LOP providers to reduce their lien — maximizing what the client takes home. This is a standard part of the disbursement process. Always send lien reduction letters and track responses.

Section 3

Police Reports — Getting & Reading Them

📋 Why Police Reports Matter

  • The police report is often the first official record of what happened
  • Used to establish liability, confirm driver identities, and get insurance info
  • Insurance companies routinely request a copy early in the claim
  • If liability is disputed, the police report can be critical evidence
  • Always get the report — even if you think you know what happened
1
Get the report number from the client
Ask: "Did police come to the scene? Do you have a report number?" If not, get the approximate date, location, and which department responded.
2
Request online or by phone
Most Atlanta-area police departments (APD, Fulton County, Cobb, DeKalb, Gwinnett) have online portals for crash reports. Georgia State Patrol: www.gsp.georgia.gov. Some require a fee ($5–$15).
3
What to look for on the report
At-fault driver's name, DOB, insurance info. Officer's narrative (who they think caused the accident). Any citations issued. Diagram of the crash. Witness names and contact info.
4
Upload to CasePeer immediately
Once received, upload to the case file the same day. Flag anything important — like a citation against the at-fault driver, or witness info we haven't contacted yet.

⚠️ When There Is No Police Report

Some accidents — especially minor ones or parking lot incidents — may not have a police report. That's okay. Document what we have: client's account, photos, witness statements. The absence of a police report doesn't kill a case, but it does make liability harder to prove. Flag it to the CM.

Section 4

Requesting Medical Records

🏥 Why We Request Records

  • Medical records document the injuries and treatment — they are the backbone of the demand
  • Insurance companies pay based on what's documented, not what the client says
  • No records = no demand = no settlement
  • Every provider the client saw must be requested
1
Get a signed HIPAA authorization from the client
HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) requires a signed authorization before any provider releases records to us. Get this signed at intake. One authorization per provider (usually).
2
Identify every provider
Ask the client: ER visit, urgent care, primary care, specialists, chiropractors, physical therapists, pain management, imaging centers. Get all names, addresses, and dates of treatment.
3
Send the request with HIPAA auth
Request both records AND bills (itemized billing). Some providers use record retrieval services (MRO, IOD, Sharecare). Follow their process. Document the date sent in CasePeer.
4
Follow up every 14 days
Providers are slow. Set a 14-day follow-up task for every outstanding request. Be polite but persistent. Log every follow-up call with date, time, and who you spoke to.
5
Upload and organize when received
Upload to CasePeer immediately. Note: are there any obvious treatment gaps? Any records that seem to be missing? Flag anything unusual to the CM.

🚨 What You Request: Records AND Bills

Never request just the records OR just the bills — always request both together. Records show what happened medically. Bills show the economic damages. Both are required for the demand package. A provider that sends only records must be called again for the billing.

Section 5

CasePeer Templates & the 14-Day Follow-Up Rule

📋 CasePeer Templates — What They Are

  • Pre-built letter and form templates inside CasePeer for common tasks
  • Saves time — you don't write from scratch every time
  • Ensures consistency — every letter is formatted correctly
  • Templates exist for: LOR, records requests, follow-up letters, lien reduction letters, and more
  • The "123-template system" means each task has a numbered template — find it by number, fill in client details, send

✅ How to Use Templates

In CasePeer, go to the case → Documents → Templates. Select the appropriate template by name or number. Fill in the variable fields (client name, provider name, accident date, etc.). Review before sending. Every template should be personalized — don't send one with blank fields.

🚨 The 14-Day Follow-Up Rule — Non-Negotiable

Every outstanding records request — and every unanswered communication — gets a follow-up every 14 days. No exceptions. Set the task in CasePeer the moment you send the request. When you follow up, log it. If a provider is unresponsive after 3 follow-ups, escalate to the CM.

📅
Day 1 — Send request
Send records request with HIPAA auth. Log in CasePeer. Set 14-day follow-up task.
📅
Day 14 — First follow-up
Call or email the provider. "Checking on our records request sent [date] for [client name]." Log the call. Set another 14-day task.
📅
Day 28 — Second follow-up
Same process. If you get a different person, re-explain. Ask for an ETA on delivery. Note it in CasePeer.
📅
Day 42+ — Escalate
Three follow-ups with no result = escalate to the CM. They may need to issue a subpoena or use a different approach.

Records don't arrive on their own. You make them arrive.

Consistent follow-up is the difference between a case that moves and a case that stalls. The 14-day rule is how we keep cases on track.
