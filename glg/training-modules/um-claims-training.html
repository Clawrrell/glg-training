MODULE 1

What is UM Coverage?

Uninsured Motorist (UM) and Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage protects our clients when they're injured by a driver who has no insurance — or not enough insurance — to cover their damages.

Governing Law

UM coverage in Georgia is governed by O.C.G.A. § 33-7-11. This is the single most important statute for UM claims in our practice.

UM is MANDATORY in Georgia. Every auto policy must include UM coverage unless the insured rejects it in writing. If the insurer cannot produce that written rejection, the insured HAS UM coverage — period.

Key Rules

  • No premium penalty: Filing a UM claim cannot result in premium increases for the insured. This is important to tell clients who are worried about using their own coverage.
  • Minimum limits: $25,000 / $50,000 / $25,000 — the same as Georgia's liability minimums (per person / per accident / property damage).
  • Matching limits: UM limits should match the insured's liability limits unless the insured specifically chose lower UM limits.
Practice Tip: Always request the full declarations page. If UM limits are lower than liability limits, investigate whether the insured actually elected the reduction in writing.
MODULE 2

When Does UM Apply?

UM coverage applies in four scenarios. Know these cold — they come up on every case.

The Four Triggers

  1. No Insurance: The at-fault driver has no liability insurance at all.
  2. Insufficient Insurance: The at-fault driver's insurance is not enough to cover our client's damages (underinsured).
  3. Hit-and-Run / Unknown Driver: The at-fault driver is unidentified. Requires physical contact with the unknown vehicle OR an independent eyewitness plus a timely accident report.
  4. Coverage Denied / Insolvency: The at-fault driver's insurer denies coverage or becomes insolvent.
Hit-and-run without physical contact is the hardest scenario. Without contact, you MUST have an independent eyewitness (not the claimant or a passenger) AND a timely police report. Missing either one can kill the claim.
Key Legal Point: Under Georgia law, "uninsured" legally includes underinsured drivers. This is critical — it means UM coverage kicks in whenever the at-fault driver's coverage is inadequate, not just when they have zero coverage.
MODULE 3

Add-On vs. Reduced-By Coverage

Georgia has two types of UM coverage. Understanding the difference can mean tens of thousands of dollars for our clients.

Add-On (Stacking) — THE DEFAULT

Since 2009: Add-on is the default under Georgia law. The insured must reject add-on in writing to get reduced-by. If the insurer can't produce that written election, the insured has add-on coverage.

With add-on coverage, UM limits are added on top of the at-fault driver's liability coverage.

Reduced-By (Traditional / Offset)

With reduced-by coverage, UM is reduced dollar-for-dollar by whatever the at-fault driver's liability pays. Your UM only covers the difference.

Stacking Rules

Stacking lets clients combine UM coverage from multiple sources. This can dramatically increase available recovery. There are two types.

Vertical Stacking

Multiple vehicles on ONE policy

When a client insures 2+ vehicles on the same policy, each vehicle's UM coverage can stack.

Stacking Rules to Remember

  • Stacking ONLY applies to UM/UIM bodily injury coverage — you cannot stack collision, property damage, or other coverages.
  • Only add-on UM can stack — reduced-by coverage has different stacking rules.

Priority Order (When Multiple UM Policies Apply)

Georgia law establishes a priority when multiple UM policies could cover a claim:

  1. Insured's own UM policy pays first
  2. Resident relatives' UM policies
  3. UM on the vehicle the insured was occupying

Courts use three tests to determine priority: the "receipt of premium" test, the "more closely identified with" test, and the "circumstances of the injury" test.

Anti-Stacking Clauses: Some insurers include language in their policies attempting to prevent stacking. Georgia courts have historically prohibited these clauses. If you spot one — flag it and challenge it. Do NOT accept it at face value.
MODULE 5

Practical Steps for GLG Staff

These are the steps you should follow on every single case. No exceptions.

  1. At Intake — Get the Dec Page: Ask the client for their full auto insurance declarations page. Check for UM coverage type (add-on or reduced-by) and limits. This is step one, every time.
  2. Identify ALL Possible UM Sources: Look beyond the client's own policy. Check:
    • Client's own auto policy
    • Resident relatives' policies
    • Policy on the vehicle client was occupying
    • Umbrella policies (sometimes include UM)
  3. Put ALL UM Carriers on Notice: Send written notice to every identified UM carrier immediately. Don't wait. Early notice protects the claim and starts the clock.
  4. Verify Add-On vs. Reduced-By: If an insurer claims the client has reduced-by coverage, demand proof of the written election. No proof = add-on coverage by default.
  5. Verify UM Wasn't Rejected: If an insurer says the client rejected UM coverage, demand proof of the written rejection. No signed rejection form = UM coverage exists.
  6. Stack When Possible: Calculate total available UM coverage across all applicable policies. Use both vertical and horizontal stacking to maximize recovery.
  7. Bad Faith Deadline — 60 Days: After sending a demand to the UM insurer, they have a 60-day window before bad faith penalties apply. Penalties can be up to 25% of recovery or $25,000 (whichever is greater), plus attorney's fees.
  8. Use a Limited Liability Release: When settling with the at-fault driver's insurer, always use a Limited Liability Release — NEVER a general release. A general release can destroy the client's UM claim.
CRITICAL: Using a general release instead of a Limited Liability Release when settling with the at-fault driver's insurer is one of the most costly mistakes in PI practice. It can waive the entire UM claim. Always use a Limited Liability Release to preserve UM rights.
