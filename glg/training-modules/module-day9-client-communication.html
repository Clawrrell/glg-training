The 14-Day Rule & CasePeer Standards

If it's not logged, it didn't happen.
📋 Keisha Williams — The Violation

You're staring at Keisha's file. 17 days without contact. A note that says "Called client 5/27 - she's good." She's texting asking if anyone is working her case.

The 14-day rule isn't a suggestion — it's the minimum standard for pre-litigation cases. And that CasePeer note? It's nearly useless. No method, no what was discussed, no next steps. If you handed this file to a colleague right now, they'd have no idea what happened or what to do next.

📞 Contact Frequency Standards

  • Pre-Litigation (before lawsuit filed): Contact the client every 14 days minimum — even if nothing is new. Check in, confirm treatment, update on timeline.
  • Litigation (after lawsuit filed): Contact every 30 days minimum — update on litigation milestones.
  • Settlement offer received: Call within 24 hours
  • Demand sent: Notify within 48 hours
  • Incoming client call/voicemail: Return within 24 hours on business days

📝 CasePeer Note Format — Required Every Time

Every communication note must include all five elements:

❌ BAD NOTE:
"Called client 5/27 - she's good."

Why it's useless: No method. No phone number. No what was discussed. No next steps. If you're ever in litigation and a client claims we never called, this note proves nothing.
✅ GOOD NOTE:
6/13/2026 — Phone (outbound) to Keisha Williams at (404) 555-1234. Called for 14-day treatment check-in. Client reports knee still painful, attending bi-weekly chiropractic at Atlanta Chiro Associates. Asked about case timeline — explained still in treatment phase, demand not yet sent. Client asked about cost of treatment — confirmed LOP, no out-of-pocket. Sent follow-up text after call. Next: set task for 6/27 — next 14-day check-in.
📱 Follow-Up Text Rule: After EVERY call attempt (answered OR not), send a follow-up text immediately. Texts create a written record and clients see them far more than voicemails.

The Welcome Call & Treatment Check-In

First impression. Ongoing relationship. Both require a script.
📋 Keisha Williams — Three Months Earlier

Let's go back to Keisha's first day as a client. The welcome call is the single most important impression the firm makes. Get it right and you set up a trusting, compliant relationship. Get it wrong and you spend the next six months managing a client who has no idea what's happening.

And every 14 days after that welcome call — the treatment check-in — is how you keep that trust alive.

👋
New Client Welcome Call — Key Steps
1
Introduce yourself — name, role (case manager), you're their point of contact start to finish
2
Explain the process — treatment phase → records collection → demand → negotiation → settlement. Be realistic about timelines.
3
Confirm treatment — is the client treating? If not, issue a LOP referral IMMEDIATELY during the call
4
Explain LOP — providers are paid from the settlement. Zero out-of-pocket right now.
5
Collect missing info — adverse insurance, own insurance carrier, police report, photos, prior injuries
6
Set communication expectations — 24-hour callback commitment. Client must return our calls promptly.
7
Document in CasePeer SAME DAY — date, type, what was covered, next steps
🚫 NEVER on a welcome call: Promise a specific settlement amount. Predict exactly how long the case will take. Discuss liability details without attorney review.
🩺
Treatment Check-In Call — Before You Dial
✓
Pull from CasePeer: client name, treating provider(s), last known appointment date, prior call notes, current case status
✓
Ask about: how they're feeling, missed appointments, any new symptoms
✓
If new symptoms: tell them to inform doctor next visit + flag for attorney review
✓
If gap identified (2+ weeks): switch to Treatment Gap Script

Treatment Gaps & Non-Responsive Clients

Insurance companies are watching. Every gap costs the client money.
📋 Keisha Williams — Month Three

Your records show Keisha's last chiropractic appointment was 23 days ago. No explanation in the notes. No voicemail, no text, no call since. You've been managing other cases and let this one slip. Now you're looking at a treatment gap that insurance will weaponize.

A two-week gap with no explanation signals "minor injury" to an adjuster. A three-week gap starts costing money. A month-long gap can destroy a case value entirely.

2 WK
2-Week Gap: Trigger Point
Switch from Treatment Check-In Script to Treatment Gap Call Script. Find out why and solve it.
30d
30-Day Gap: Serious Red Flag
Escalate to attorney IMMEDIATELY. Attorney decides whether to continue, adjust strategy, or begin case review.
ATT
Client Wants to Stop: Always Escalate
Never accept a client's decision to stop treatment without attorney approval. Always escalate to Harrell or Adriauna.

🚫 Three Unanswered Attempts — The Protocol

  • Attempt 1: Call → voicemail → text → log → set task for 3 business days
  • Attempt 2: Call again (try different time) → voicemail → text → try alternate number → log → set task
  • Attempt 3: Send formal letter AND email — "We have been trying to reach you... please contact us within 10 days." Set task for 10 business days.
  • Still no response: Flag to supervisor → supervisor/attorney decides to continue or begin case close review
💡 Common Gap Reasons — Always Ask Before Lecturing:
Transportation issues → Help coordinate. Billing confusion → Correct it (LOP means $0 out of pocket). Scheduling conflict → Find different time/provider. Feeling better → Explain consequences, escalate to attorney.

Demand Update & Settlement Offer Calls

Big milestones. High stakes. Specific scripts for a reason.
📋 Keisha Williams — Six Months Later

Keisha got back on track with treatment. The demand was sent last week. Now the insurance company has responded with an offer. These are the two most important calls you'll make in a client's case — and they both have precise scripts for a reason.

Mess up the demand update call and she talks to the adjuster directly. Mess up the settlement offer call and she makes a life-altering financial decision without full information. Both mistakes create legal exposure for the firm.

📬
Demand Update Call — Key Points
1
Call proactively — don't wait for the client to ask. "I'm calling with an update — good news!"
2
Explain what a demand IS in plain English (not legal jargon)
3
Set the 30-day response window expectation — may take longer
⚠
CRITICAL: "Do NOT speak to the insurance company directly. Tell them you are represented by Gunn Law Group and to call us."
💰
Settlement Offer Call — Must-Haves Before You Dial
✓
The offer amount from the insurance company
✓
Estimated attorney fee (per fee agreement)
✓
Estimated case expenses (records fees, costs advanced)
✓
Estimated net to client (after all deductions)
⚠
MANDATORY: The attorney's recommendation — accept or counter, and why. You cannot present an offer without this.
🚨 Never pressure a client to accept or reject. Present the facts neutrally. Present the attorney's recommendation and reasoning. If the client is upset about the offer, escalate to Harrell or Adriauna immediately — do not argue about the number.

Difficult Conversations & When to Escalate

You won't always have easy conversations. Know what to say — and what never to say.
📋 Keisha Williams — It Gets Hard

The settlement offer came in lower than Keisha expected. She's been waiting eight months. She has questions she's not getting answered. And she's starting to get angry. This is where you earn your keep — or where the case goes sideways.

Every difficult conversation has a protocol. The worst thing you can do is improvise when you're flustered. Know these responses cold.

💬 Core Principle: Restore Safety Before Content

When a client is upset, they cannot process information. Your first job is always to restore their sense that you are on their side. Lead with acknowledgment, not explanation. Validate the emotion before you address the content. Never get defensive.

🚨 When to Escalate to the Attorney (Non-Negotiable)

⬆ Client threatens to fire the firm or transfer the case — attorney must be involved immediately
⬆ Client wants to stop treatment — never accept without attorney approval
⬆ Client is upset about the settlement offer — don't argue numbers; escalate
⬆ Fee dispute continues after your explanation — escalate to attorney
⬆ Client has not treated in 30+ days and is unresponsive — attorney decides next steps
⬆ Client mentions a new accident or new serious injury — flag immediately
🚫 NEVER SAY (in any difficult conversation):
"That's not my department." · "That's not my fault." · "I already told you that." · "You need to calm down." · "There's nothing I can do right now." · "No other attorney will take your case."
