Section 1

How to Verify Insurance Coverage

One of your most important early tasks on every new case: verify insurance coverage for all parties. You need to know who's insured, with whom, and for how much — before you can open a claim or advise on strategy.

📋 Information You Need to Collect

For the At-Fault DriverFor Our Client
Insurance company nameInsurance company name
Policy numberPolicy number
Claim number (if already opened)UM/UIM coverage & limits
Adjuster name & phoneMedPay coverage & limits
BI policy limits (30-day rule applies)Health insurance provider & policy #
PD coverage infoAny other auto policies (stacking)

📞 How to Verify Coverage — Step by Step

1
Get the at-fault driver's info from the client or police report
Name, license plate, insurance company name (from the declarations page or insurance card). The police report often has this.
2
Call the insurance company's claims line
Tell them: "I'm calling on behalf of [client name], who was involved in an accident with your insured [at-fault driver name] on [date]. I'd like to verify coverage and open a claim."
3
Confirm coverage exists for the date of loss
The key question: "Can you confirm your insured had active coverage on [date of accident]?" They will tell you yes or no. They won't give you limits on the first call — that requires a written request.
4
Get an assigned adjuster and claim number
Ask: "Who will be handling this claim and what is the claim number?" Log this in CasePeer immediately.
5
Send the LOR and written policy limits request same day
Your LOR establishes representation. The written policy limits request triggers the 30-day clock under O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28.

⚠️ Never Rely on Verbal Confirmations for Limits

An adjuster may tell you the limits over the phone — but the written request is what starts the 30-day legal clock. Always send the written limits request. Always. Even if they told you the limits verbally, send the letter.

Section 2

O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28 — The Policy Limits Disclosure Law

This Georgia law is one of the most important statutes you'll work with. Know it cold.

O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28

What the Law Says

In any liability insurance claim, upon written request by or on behalf of a claimant, the insurer must disclose the policy limits within 30 days of receiving the written request. Failure to do so can result in consequences for the insurer at trial.

✅ What Triggers the 30-Day Clock
  • A written request (letter or email) to the insurer
  • Must specifically request "policy limits" or "limits of liability"
  • Must be signed by the attorney or authorized representative
  • Clock starts from the date insurer receives it
❌ What Doesn't Count
  • Verbal requests over the phone
  • General LOR without a specific limits request
  • Requests made before hiring an attorney
  • Requests from the client directly (must be attorney or rep)

📅 Why the 30-Day Timeline Matters

  • Knowing the limits early lets us evaluate whether to settle within policy or pursue a bad faith claim
  • If limits are low, we can prepare a time-limited demand to create additional pressure
  • If the insurer refuses or delays, that failure can be used against them at trial
  • It prevents the insurer from hiding "low limits" to lowball early settlement offers

✅ Track the 30-Day Deadline in CasePeer

The day you send the written limits request, create a task in CasePeer 30 days out: "Limits response due — O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28." If no response by then, flag it to the attorney immediately.

Sample Language for Written Limits Request "Pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28, please provide the full declaration pages and all applicable policy limits for your insured, [at-fault driver name], including bodily injury, underinsured motorist, umbrella, and any other applicable coverages, for the accident occurring on [date] involving our client, [client name]."
Section 3

Opening a Claim with the Insurance Company

Opening a claim is one of the first things that happens on every new case. Here's exactly what you need and what to say.

📋 What You Need Before You Call

  • Client's full name and date of birth
  • Date, time, and location of the accident
  • At-fault driver's name and insurance company
  • At-fault driver's policy number (if available)
  • Brief description of the accident (client's version)
  • Our firm's name and contact information
  • Signed LOR (or the intent to send it immediately after the call)
Opening Call Script "Hello, my name is [your name] and I'm calling from Gunn Law Group in Atlanta, Georgia. We represent [client name] in connection with an accident that occurred on [date] in [location] involving your insured, [at-fault driver name].

I'd like to open a claim and get an assigned adjuster. Can you confirm your insured had coverage on that date?

[After confirmation]: Thank you. We'll be sending our letter of representation shortly. What is the claim number and who will be handling this claim?"

📝 What to Log After the Call

  • Claim number (critical — every future communication uses this)
  • Assigned adjuster's name and direct phone number
  • Date and time of the call
  • Whether coverage was confirmed for the date of loss
  • Any additional information they provided

Log all of this in CasePeer immediately after hanging up. Then send the LOR and written limits request same day.

⚠️ What the Adjuster May Ask You

  • "Can we get a recorded statement from your client?" — Answer: "No, all communication with our client goes through our office. We'll send you our letter of representation."
  • "Can you give me more details about the accident?" — Provide only basic facts (date, location, general description). Do not share client's injuries or treatment.
  • "Can we have the client's medical records now?" — Answer: "Those will be provided through the appropriate channels in due course."
Section 4

Claim Numbers, Denials & No Insurance Situations

The claim number is the backbone of every insurance interaction. And sometimes — the at-fault driver claims they have no insurance. Here's how to handle both.

🔢 Why Claim Numbers Matter

  • Every insurer assigns a unique claim number when you open a claim
  • It's the ID for that specific accident at that specific insurance company
  • Without it, adjusters can't locate the file — every call takes longer
  • Always lead with the claim number when calling an insurer
  • Enter it in CasePeer immediately — it goes on every letter, fax, and communication
  • You may have multiple claim numbers for one case (BI claim + PD claim, or multiple insurers)

❌ "They Said They Have No Insurance"

This happens more than you'd think. Here's what to do when coverage is denied or the driver claims no insurance:

1
Don't take the driver's word for it
At-fault drivers sometimes lie about having insurance. Get the license plate number and run a check. The police report often has the insurance info even if the driver didn't volunteer it.
2
Check the client's own policy for UM coverage
If the at-fault driver truly has no insurance, we pursue an Uninsured Motorist (UM) claim through the client's own policy. This is exactly what UM coverage exists for. Always verify our client's UM coverage early.
3
Check if the at-fault driver has a different insurer
Sometimes the insurer says "no coverage on that date" — meaning the policy lapsed. Get more info: when did it lapse? Was there another policy? Flag to the attorney.
4
Flag all denial situations to the attorney immediately
A coverage denial is a legal issue, not just an administrative one. Log what you were told, who you spoke with, and when — then hand it off to the CM and attorney immediately.

⚠️ Coverage Denial ≠ Case Over

A denial of coverage does not mean the case is over. It means we need to investigate further. Our client still has rights — through UM coverage, through other parties, or through legal action directly against the at-fault driver. Always flag, never assume.

Section 5

Coordinating Multiple Coverages on One Case

Many PI cases involve more than one source of coverage. Understanding how multiple coverages interact is essential — and missing one can cost the client real money.

🔄 Common Multi-Coverage Scenarios

1
At-Fault Driver's BI + Client's UIM
Most common scenario. We pursue the at-fault driver's BI first. If their limits are exhausted and our client's damages exceed those limits, we then pursue UIM on our client's own policy. Example: At-fault has $25K BI. Client's damages are $80K. We settle BI for $25K, then pursue $55K from UIM.
2
MedPay + BI
MedPay pays medical bills immediately (regardless of fault). At settlement, the MedPay insurer has a subrogation right — they want to be reimbursed from the BI settlement. Track MedPay payments and balances carefully. They will submit a lien at disbursement.
3
Health Insurance + BI
If the client's health insurance paid for treatment, health insurance may also have a subrogation right. This is more complex — some policies have full subrogation, some are reduced by attorney fees. The attorney handles this at disbursement, but you need to know it exists.
4
Multiple At-Fault Parties
Some accidents involve multiple at-fault parties (e.g., two drivers, or a driver + a property owner). Each party has their own insurer, their own claim number, and their own limits. You may be managing multiple parallel claims on the same case.
5
UM Stacking Across Multiple Policies
If the client has multiple vehicles on their policy — or policies on multiple vehicles in the household — Georgia law may allow stacking of UM limits. Example: two cars, each with $50K UM = potentially $100K in UM coverage. Always check this at intake.

✅ The Coverage Checklist — Run It on Every Case

  • At-fault driver's BI and PD (and limits, via § 33-3-28 request)
  • Client's UM/UIM coverage and limits
  • Client's MedPay coverage and limits
  • Client's health insurance (company, policy number, potential subrogation)
  • Any umbrella or excess policies held by the at-fault driver
  • Any other vehicles in client's household with their own policies (stacking opportunity)
  • Workers' comp if the accident happened during work (creates its own lien issues)

Every source of coverage is money for the client.

Missing coverage at intake means missing recovery at settlement. Run the full checklist every time. You're not just a paper-pusher — you're protecting the client's financial recovery.
