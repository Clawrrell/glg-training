How Insurance Works in Personal Injury

Insurance is the foundation of every PI case. Understanding how it works — and how insurance companies think — is critical to representing clients effectively.

🐊 The Truth About Insurance

Insurance companies are for-profit businesses first. Their primary duty is to shareholders, not claimants or policyholders. They make money by collecting premiums and paying out as little as possible in claims. Every dollar they deny or delay is profit. This isn't illegal — it's their business model. Understanding this shapes how we approach every claim.

📈 Insurance Company Goals
  • Minimize payouts on every claim
  • Delay settlements to reduce present value
  • Dispute liability whenever possible
  • Question medical necessity and causation
  • Use recorded statements to find contradictions
  • Protect shareholders' profits above all else
⚖️ Our Client's Goals
  • Fair compensation for all losses
  • Medical bills paid quickly
  • Return to pre-accident life
  • Justice and accountability
  • Peace of mind and security
  • Community safety (others protected from same harm)

🎯 The Client-Centered Approach

Per our Client-Centered training, clients don't just buy legal services — they buy the experience of receiving justice. When explaining insurance to clients:

  • Use plain language, not insurance jargon
  • Explain what each coverage means to them personally
  • Set realistic expectations about timing and process
  • Frame everything around their recovery and protection
  • Show empathy for their frustration with insurance delays

⚠️ Never Assume Clients Understand Insurance

Most people pay premiums for years without truly understanding what they bought. Terms like "BI," "UM/UIM," and "policy limits" are foreign to them. Our job is to be their guide and translator, not to assume they know what we're talking about.

Section 2

Types of Auto Insurance Coverage

Every auto insurance policy has multiple types of coverage. Understanding each type and how they interact is essential for maximizing client recovery.

🏥 Bodily Injury (BI) — Third Party
  • Who pays: At-fault driver's insurance company
  • What it covers: Injuries to other people caused by their insured
  • This is our primary target in most PI cases
  • Includes: Medical bills, lost wages, pain & suffering, future damages
  • Key limit: Per-person maximum (e.g., $25,000 per person)
🚗 Property Damage (PD) — Third Party
  • Who pays: At-fault driver's insurance company
  • What it covers: Vehicle damage, property damage
  • Separate from injury claim — different adjuster often
  • Usually resolves first: Car repair/replacement happens quickly
  • Not our primary focus but track in CasePeer
🚫 Uninsured Motorist (UM) — First Party
  • Who pays: Our client's own insurance company
  • When it applies: At-fault driver has no insurance
  • Also applies to: Hit-and-run accidents
  • Functions like BI: Covers medical, lost wages, pain & suffering
  • Critical coverage that many clients don't know they have
⬇️ Underinsured Motorist (UIM) — First Party
  • Who pays: Our client's own insurance company
  • When it applies: At-fault driver's limits insufficient for damages
  • Example: $100K damages, only $25K BI → UIM pays the gap
  • Often the largest recovery source in serious injury cases
  • Can "stack" in Georgia under certain circumstances
💊 Medical Payments (MedPay) — First Party
  • Who pays: Our client's own insurance company
  • No fault required: Pays regardless of who caused accident
  • Fast payment: No liability investigation needed
  • Common amounts: $2,000 / $5,000 / $10,000
  • Watch for subrogation: May want repayment from BI settlement
🏥 Personal Injury Protection (PIP) — First Party
  • Who pays: Our client's own insurance company
  • Not common in Georgia — but appears in some policies
  • Broader than MedPay: Covers medical + lost wages
  • No-fault benefit: Pays regardless of liability
  • Subrogation applies: Usually wants repayment from settlement

✅ Systematic Coverage Verification Process

Every new case gets the same systematic approach: (1) Identify all available coverage — BI, UM/UIM, MedPay, PIP; (2) Verify policy limits for each coverage; (3) Document everything in CasePeer with dates and contact info; (4) Flag any gaps or issues to the case manager immediately. This system ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

Section 3

Georgia Insurance Requirements & Minimums

Georgia law requires all drivers to carry minimum insurance, but these minimums are often inadequate for serious injuries. Understanding Georgia's requirements helps us identify coverage gaps and pursue all available sources of recovery.

🏛️ Georgia's Mandatory Minimums (25/50/25)

  • $25,000 bodily injury per person
  • $50,000 bodily injury per accident total
  • $25,000 property damage per accident
  • Uninsured motorist coverage required (can be rejected in writing)
  • Underinsured motorist coverage available (optional add-on)

🚨 The Adequacy Crisis

Georgia's 25/50/25 minimums haven't increased since 1991, while medical costs have skyrocketed. A single ER visit can exceed $25,000. One day in ICU can cost $10,000+. Most drivers carrying minimum limits are essentially uninsured for any serious injury. This is why UM/UIM coverage is critical — it fills the gap when at-fault drivers are underinsured.

🐊 Community Safety Rules

Insurance requirements exist to protect the entire community. When drivers carry inadequate insurance, they're violating the community's safety rules. A driver with $25,000 coverage who causes $100,000 in damages has failed in their responsibility to the community. Every Georgia driver should carry enough insurance to handle the worst-case scenario they might cause — because it could happen to anyone, including the jurors themselves.

1
Georgia Requirement: BI Coverage
Every driver must carry $25,000/$50,000 bodily injury coverage minimum. Higher limits are encouraged but not required.
2
Georgia Requirement: PD Coverage
$25,000 property damage coverage minimum. Covers damage to other vehicles and property (not the driver's own car).
3
Georgia Requirement: UM Coverage
Uninsured motorist coverage is required unless rejected in writing. Must equal the BI limits selected.
4
Georgia Option: UIM Coverage
Underinsured motorist coverage is optional but highly valuable. Can exceed BI limits in many cases.

📌 UM/UIM Stacking in Georgia

Georgia allows "stacking" of UM/UIM policies in certain situations. If a client has multiple vehicles or policies, the limits may combine to create higher total coverage. Examples: (1) Client has two vehicles with $50K UM each → potentially $100K available; (2) Client has both auto and umbrella policy with UM → may stack. Always investigate stacking opportunities — they can significantly increase recovery.

Section 4

Client-Centered Insurance Communication

How we explain insurance to clients directly impacts their experience with GLG. Our approach should be educational, empathetic, and empowering — never condescending or rushed.

🎯 Client-Centered Explanation Framework

Start with what matters to them: "Let me explain how insurance works and what this means for your recovery."

  • Use analogies: "Insurance is like a pool of money the company sets aside for accidents"
  • Explain personal impact: "This coverage means your medical bills can be paid"
  • Set expectations: "Here's what we expect to happen and when"
  • Address fears: "I know dealing with insurance companies is frustrating — that's why you have us"

✅ Script: Explaining BI Coverage to Clients

"Mrs. Johnson, the at-fault driver has $50,000 in bodily injury coverage. Think of this like a bank account set aside for people hurt in accidents caused by their driver. This $50,000 is what's available to pay for your medical bills, lost wages, and compensation for your pain and suffering. Our job is to make sure you get every dollar you deserve from that pool of money, and to explore any other coverage that might be available to you."

✅ Script: Explaining UM/UIM to Clients

"Mr. Davis, you have something called Uninsured Motorist coverage on your own policy. This is like an insurance policy on your insurance policy. If the person who hit you doesn't have insurance, or doesn't have enough insurance, your own company steps in to fill the gap. The good news is, you've been paying for this coverage for years — now it's time for it to protect you the way it was designed to."

🚨 What NOT to Say to Clients

  • "It's complicated" or "You wouldn't understand" — This is dismissive
  • "We'll deal with the insurance" — Clients want to know what's happening
  • "The insurance company is evil" — Stay professional, explain business motives
  • "You only have minimum coverage" — Don't shame clients for coverage levels
  • "This will take forever" — Be realistic but not discouraging
1
Acknowledge Their Situation
"I know this is overwhelming. You're dealing with injuries, and now insurance complications on top of it."
2
Explain in Simple Terms
Use plain language and analogies. Check understanding: "Does that make sense so far?"
3
Connect to Their Recovery
"This coverage means we have $X available to cover your medical bills and compensate you for what you've been through."
4
Set Clear Expectations
"Here's what happens next and approximately when. I'll keep you updated every step of the way."
5
Reassure and Empower
"You don't have to deal with insurance companies anymore — that's our job. Focus on your recovery."

Insurance is confusing by design. Our job is to translate confusion into clarity.

Clients judge us not just on results, but on how well we guide them through the process.

Section 5

Understanding Adjuster Tactics & Motivations

Insurance adjusters have specific tactics designed to minimize payouts. Understanding their playbook helps us counter their strategies and protect our clients' interests.

🎯 The Adjuster's Job (From Our Negotiation Framework)

  • Minimize claim value through every available method
  • Close files quickly to meet performance metrics
  • Gather information that can be used to deny or reduce claims
  • Document everything that supports their company's position
  • Appear reasonable and helpful while protecting company profits

🚨 Common Adjuster Tactics to Expect

  • Recorded statements: "We just need to get your version of events" — seeking inconsistencies
  • Quick settlement offers: Low-ball offers before medical treatment is complete
  • Medical authorization requests: Seeking access to entire medical history
  • Surveillance: Watching for activities inconsistent with claimed injuries
  • Delay tactics: "We need more information" / "We're still investigating"
  • Blame the victim: Suggesting client contributed to the accident

🐊 The Counter-Strategy

Every adjuster tactic prioritizes company profits over community safety. When adjusters delay payment for legitimate medical bills, they force injured people to choose between treatment and financial hardship. This violates the fundamental community safety rule that those who cause harm should make it right promptly. Frame every adjuster interaction around: "What safety rule is being violated here, and how does that harm the community?"

1
TACTIC: Early Recorded Statements
Their goal: Get client on record before they understand their injuries or legal rights. Our response: "All communication goes through counsel. No recorded statement without attorney present."
2
TACTIC: Quick Low-Ball Offers
Their goal: Settle before true extent of injuries is known. Our response: "Client's medical treatment is ongoing. Demand will be submitted when maximum medical improvement is reached."
3
TACTIC: Broad Medical Authorizations
Their goal: Access to lifetime medical records to find pre-existing conditions. Our response: Provide only accident-related records and limit authorizations by date and provider.
4
TACTIC: Disputes Over Medical Necessity
Their goal: Claim treatment was unnecessary or excessive. Our response: Strong medical documentation and narrative reports from treating physicians.
5
TACTIC: Policy Limits Games
Their goal: Refuse to disclose limits or claim "we don't discuss policy limits." Our response: O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28 requires disclosure within 30 days of written request.

✅ GLG's Adjuster Relationship Strategy

While we understand adjusters work for profit-driven companies, we maintain professional relationships. Be courteous and responsive, but never compromise on the client's interests. If an adjuster is unreasonable or engages in bad-faith tactics, document everything and escalate to the supervising attorney immediately. Professional courtesy combined with firm boundaries gets better results than hostility.
