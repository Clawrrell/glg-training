Section 1

Bodily Injury (BI) vs. Property Damage (PD)

When someone is at fault in an accident, their auto insurance has two main coverages that matter to us:

🏥 Bodily Injury (BI)
  • Pays for injuries to other people caused by the at-fault driver
  • This is our primary target in PI cases
  • Covers medical bills, lost wages, pain & suffering
  • Paid by the at-fault driver's insurance company
  • Example: At-fault driver has 25/50 BI → max $25,000 per person
🚗 Property Damage (PD)
  • Pays for damage to vehicles or property
  • Handled separately from the injury claim
  • Usually resolved quickly (car repair/total loss)
  • Not our primary focus — but still tracked in CasePeer
  • Example: At-fault driver's PD coverage pays to fix our client's car

⚠️ Key Distinction

BI = who pays for the injuries. PD = who pays for the car. These are separate claims, sometimes with separate adjusters. Never confuse them when talking to insurance companies.

📖 BI Coverage Format: 25/50/25

  • First number (25): Max per person per accident — $25,000
  • Second number (50): Max per accident total — $50,000
  • Third number (25): Property damage max — $25,000
  • Georgia minimum requirement: 25/50/25
  • Always ask: "What are the bodily injury limits?" when opening a claim
Section 2

Uninsured & Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM)

🚫 Uninsured Motorist (UM)

  • The at-fault driver has no insurance at all
  • Our client makes a claim on their own UM coverage
  • Our client's insurance company pays as if they were the at-fault driver
  • Hit-and-run accidents also trigger UM coverage
  • Always check: does our client have UM coverage? How much?

⬇️ Underinsured Motorist (UIM)

  • The at-fault driver has insurance, but not enough to cover all the damages
  • Example: Client has $100,000 in damages; at-fault driver only has $25,000 BI
  • Client's UIM coverage fills the gap: pays the difference up to their UIM limit
  • UIM is one of the most valuable coverages — and most people don't understand they have it

✅ Real-World Example

Our client suffers $80,000 in medical bills. At-fault driver has 25/50 BI. That means max payout = $25,000 from BI. If our client has $100,000 UIM, we can collect another $55,000 from their own policy (the difference). Total recovery: $80,000. Without UIM, the client is stuck at $25,000.

🚨 UM/UIM Stacking in Georgia

Georgia allows "stacking" of UM/UIM policies in some situations. If a client has multiple vehicles or multiple policies, the UM limits may stack (add together) to create a larger pool of available coverage. This can significantly increase recovery. Always flag stacking opportunities to the CM or attorney.

📌 What You Need to Do

When a new case comes in, always verify: (1) Does the at-fault driver have insurance? (2) What are their BI limits? (3) Does our client have UM/UIM? (4) What are our client's UM/UIM limits? Record everything in CasePeer.

Section 3

MedPay — The Coverage People Forget

💊 What Is MedPay?

  • Medical Payments Coverage — an optional add-on to auto insurance
  • Pays for medical expenses regardless of fault
  • Available to the insured and passengers in their vehicle
  • Pays quickly — no waiting to prove fault
  • Common amounts: $2,000 / $5,000 / $10,000
  • Does NOT affect the BI or UM/UIM claim — it's separate

✅ Why MedPay Matters

MedPay can pay for early medical treatment while the main BI claim is still being worked. It reduces the amount of unpaid medical bills our client accumulates. Think of it as a small but fast-paying safety net. Always ask if the client has MedPay on their own policy.

✅ MedPay Covers
  • ER visits
  • Doctor visits
  • Surgery
  • X-rays, MRIs
  • Physical therapy
  • Ambulance
❌ MedPay Does NOT Cover
  • Lost wages
  • Pain & suffering
  • Vehicle damage
  • Out-of-network providers (sometimes)
  • Pre-existing conditions (usually)

⚠️ MedPay Subrogation

If MedPay pays out and then our client also recovers money from the at-fault driver's BI, the client's own insurance company may have a subrogation lien — meaning they want their MedPay money back. Track any MedPay lien in CasePeer and flag it at settlement time.

Section 4

Policy Limits — The Cap on Recovery

Policy limits are the maximum amount an insurance company will pay on a claim. This is one of the most important numbers in every PI case.

🔢 How Limits Work

  • Every auto insurance policy has a per-person limit and a per-accident limit
  • No matter how severe the injuries, the insurance company will not pay more than the policy limit
  • If damages exceed the limit, the at-fault driver is personally responsible for the rest
  • Most individual drivers have low limits ($25K–$100K)
  • Commercial vehicles (trucks, rideshares) often have much higher limits ($1M+)

🚨 The Limits Problem

A client can have $200,000 in medical bills, but if the at-fault driver only has 25/50 BI, the most we can get from that policy is $25,000 per person. This is why UM/UIM coverage on the client's own policy is so critical — it fills the gap when limits are low. Always tell the attorney when limits appear insufficient for the damages.

1
Get Limits Early
Call the at-fault driver's insurance as soon as a claim is opened. Ask for policy limits. Under O.C.G.A. § 33-3-28, they must disclose within 30 days of a written request.
2
Record in CasePeer
Enter the confirmed limits in the case file — BI limits, PD limits, and any UM/UIM on our client's policy. Always note the date confirmed and who you spoke to.
3
Flag Low Limits
If limits are low relative to the severity of the case, flag it to the CM immediately. This affects case strategy — including whether to pursue UM/UIM or personal assets.
4
Policy Limit Demands
Sometimes we demand the full policy limit. If the insurer refuses and damages clearly exceed limits, that can open up bad faith claims against the insurer. That's an attorney decision — just know it exists.
Section 5

Verifying Coverage & Opening a Claim

One of your most important early tasks: verify coverage on every new case. Here's the step-by-step process:

1
Get the at-fault driver's insurance info
From the police report, client, or the claim itself. You need: insurance company name, policy number, and the insured's name and date of birth.
2
Call the insurance company
Call their claims line. Identify yourself: "This is [your name] calling from Gunn Law Group. We represent [client name] regarding an accident on [date]."
3
Open the claim (if not already done)
Provide: client's name, accident date, accident location, at-fault driver info, brief description of the accident. Get a claim number and adjuster name.
4
Ask these questions every time
"What are the bodily injury limits?" / "Is liability accepted?" / "Is there a recorded statement requirement?" / "What's the adjuster's direct line and email?"
5
Document everything in CasePeer
Log the call: date, time, who you spoke to, what was confirmed. Enter the claim number, adjuster name, and confirmed limits. This is your paper trail.

🚨 Never Let the Client Give a Recorded Statement

The insurance adjuster may call your client directly and ask for a recorded statement. Your client should never give a recorded statement without speaking to their attorney first. Advise clients immediately: all communication goes through us.

Verify coverage early. Know the limits before you need them.

An unknown insurance situation is a ticking clock. The sooner you verify, the better you can protect the client's recovery.
