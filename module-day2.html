Section 1

Types of PI Cases We Handle

Personal injury (PI) law covers many types of accidents and injuries. Here are the main case types you'll see at Gunn Law Group:

🚗 Auto Accidents ⭐ Most Common

A collision between motor vehicles — rear-end, intersection, highway, or hit-and-run. This is the majority of our cases.

Example: A driver runs a red light and T-bones our client's car at an intersection, causing neck and back injuries.
🚛 Trucking Accidents High Value

Accidents involving commercial trucks and CDL drivers, governed by FMCSA federal regulations — higher insurance limits and more complex liability.

Example: A semi-truck driver, fatigued from exceeding drive-time limits, rear-ends our client on I-285, causing serious injuries.
🏪 Premises Liability Property Owner at Fault

Injuries that occur on someone else's property due to unsafe conditions — slip and fall, negligent security. The property owner is responsible.

Example: Our client slips on a wet floor in a grocery store with no warning signs and breaks their wrist.
🚶 Pedestrian & Bicycle Accidents

Cases where a pedestrian or cyclist is struck by a motor vehicle. Injuries tend to be severe because there is no vehicle protection.

Example: Our client is riding a bicycle in a bike lane when a car door swings open, causing them to crash and suffer a broken collarbone.
🐕 Dog Bites

Injuries caused by a dog attack. In Georgia, the dog owner can be held liable if they knew the dog had dangerous tendencies.

Example: A neighbor's dog escapes the yard and bites our client, requiring stitches and causing infection.
⚖️ Wrongful Death Sensitive

Cases where someone's negligence caused another person's death. The family of the deceased brings the claim. These require extra care and sensitivity.

Example: A drunk driver kills our client's spouse in a car accident. We represent the surviving family to recover damages.
Section 2

What is Liability?

📖 Key Terms

  • Liability — who is legally at fault for the accident
  • Negligence — failing to act as a reasonable person would (e.g., running a red light)
  • At-fault — the person who caused the accident through their negligence

Not all liability is equal. Here's how it breaks down:

✅ Clear Liability

Example: Rear-end collision. The driver who hits from behind is almost always at fault. There's very little room for dispute — if you rear-ended someone, you weren't paying attention or following too closely.

⚠️ Disputed Liability

Example: Intersection accident. Both drivers may claim the other ran the red light. Without a witness or camera footage, it's harder to prove who was at fault. These cases require more investigation.

⚡ Georgia Comparative Negligence Law — O.C.G.A. § 51-12-33

In Georgia, if our client is partially at fault, their recovery is reduced by their percentage of fault.

Critical rule: If the client is 50% or more at fault, they recover NOTHING.

Example: Client is 30% at fault → they receive 70% of damages. Client is 50% at fault → they receive $0.

🎯 Our Job

  • Prove the other person was at fault
  • Prove our client was NOT at fault (or minimize their percentage)
  • Gather evidence: photos, police reports, witness statements, camera footage
  • The stronger the liability, the stronger our negotiating position
Section 3

Claim vs. Lawsuit

These two words get confused constantly. They are NOT the same thing. Here's the difference:

📋 CLAIM (Pre-Litigation)
  • Demand for money from the insurance company
  • No court involved
  • Handled by our pre-lit team
  • Faster for the client
  • Less expensive
  • Most of our cases settle here
⚖️ LAWSUIT (Litigation)
  • File a complaint with the court
  • Insurance won't pay fairly
  • Goes to Landon's team
  • Takes longer
  • More expensive
  • Sometimes necessary for a fair result

🏁 The Goal

  • Always try to resolve at the claim stage — faster + cheaper for the client
  • If the insurance company refuses to pay fairly, we file suit
  • Filing suit isn't a failure — it's a tool to get our clients what they deserve
  • When a case moves to litigation, it transfers to Landon's litigation team

📌 What This Means for You

As a Legal Assistant, you primarily work in pre-litigation (claim stage). Your goal is to help move cases forward so they resolve efficiently. If a case goes to litigation, you'll help with the handoff to Landon's team.

Section 4

How Cases Move Through CasePeer

Every case in CasePeer has a status — a label that tells the whole team exactly where the case is in the process. Here are the main statuses in order:

1
Intake
Client just called in. They have not signed our fee agreement yet. We are evaluating whether to take the case.
2
Intake Review
Client has signed. An attorney is reviewing the case details before it officially opens.
3
Treating
Client is actively going to the doctor for their injuries. We are gathering medical records and monitoring progress.
4
Pending Demand
Client has finished treatment. We are assembling all records, bills, and documentation to build the demand package.
5
Demanded
The demand letter has been sent to the insurance company. We are waiting for their response.
6
Settlement Negotiations
Insurance has made an offer. We are negotiating back and forth to reach a fair settlement.
7
Settled
Both sides have agreed on a number. The settlement is confirmed. Now we move to disbursement.
8
Disbursement
Cutting the check to the client. Paying liens, attorney fees, and distributing the client's portion. Often the most complex step.
9
Closed
Everything is paid out. The case is complete and archived.

🚨 Why Status Accuracy Matters

If a case is in the wrong status, it doesn't get the right attention.

A case stuck in "Treating" for 8 months might mean the client stopped going to the doctor — and nobody noticed. That's a problem. Cases with stale statuses fall through the cracks.

Always flag a status that looks wrong.

Section 5

Your Role in All This

As a Legal Assistant, you support the Case Manager (CM) at every stage. Here's what that looks like in practice:

1. Intake
Help set up the case in CasePeer. Enter client info, upload the signed fee agreement, confirm insurance details are captured.
2. Treating
Request medical records. Follow up every 14 days on all outstanding requests. Verify the client's health insurance coverage for lien purposes.
3. Demand
Help assemble the demand package — organize records, bills, photos, and documentation so the CM can send a complete, strong demand.
4. Settlement
Send lien reduction letters to hospitals, insurers, and government agencies. Track responses. Confirm all liens are resolved before disbursement.
5. Disbursement
Help prepare the settlement statement. Ensure all payees are identified, amounts are correct, and the client understands what they're receiving.

The CM drives. You keep it running.

Without consistent follow-up, cases stall. Your attention to detail and follow-through is what moves cases from Treating to Closed.
