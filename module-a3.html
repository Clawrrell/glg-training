What is a Personal Injury Case?

The Core Concept

A personal injury case exists when someone gets hurt because of someone else's negligence. Negligence means they failed to act as a reasonable person would — and because of that failure, our client was harmed.

⚖️ Our job is to prove: (1) someone else was at fault, and (2) our client was harmed as a result. Then we fight to get our client compensated.

Types of Cases We Handle

🚗 Auto Accidents
🚛 Trucking Crashes
🏪 Slip & Fall
🏢 Premises Liability
🐕 Dog Bites

The vast majority of our cases are auto accidents. Understanding the auto accident pipeline is essential for every employee.

Who We Represent

We represent the injured person (the Plaintiff) — never the insurance company, never the at-fault driver. We are always on our client's side.

We go up against the Defendant (the person who caused the injury) and their insurance company.

How We Get Paid — Contingency Fee

💰 We only get paid if the client gets money. Our fee is a percentage of the settlement or verdict. If we don't win, we don't collect attorney fees. This is called a contingency fee arrangement.

This means we are fully invested in every case. Our success is tied to our client's success.

Section 2 of 8
Key Terms You Need to Know

You'll hear these words every day. Know them cold.

Plaintiff
Our client. The person who was injured. We represent the plaintiff.
Defendant
The person who caused the injury. We are going against the defendant (and their insurance).
Negligence
Failing to act as a reasonable person would. A driver running a red light is negligent. A store ignoring a wet floor is negligent.
Liability
Who is at fault. "Establishing liability" means proving the other party caused the accident.
Damages
The harm our client suffered — medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, future treatment costs.
Claim
A demand for compensation made to the insurance company. Before we file a lawsuit, we make a claim.
Lawsuit / Litigation
When we take the case to court because insurance won't pay fairly. Litigation is the formal legal process.
Settlement
Both sides agree on a dollar amount without going to trial. Most cases settle. This is our goal.
Verdict
When a case goes to trial and a jury decides how much the defendant must pay.

How Insurance Works in PI

The Key Insurance Types

BI — Bodily Injury

The at-fault driver's insurance pays for our client's injuries. This is the primary claim in most cases. BI covers medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

PD — Property Damage

Covers damage to the client's car. PD is a separate claim from BI. We sometimes help coordinate, but it's distinct from the injury claim.

UM / UIM — Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist

Our client's own insurance pays when the other driver has no insurance (UM) or not enough to cover the damages (UIM). We send a separate LOR for UM claims.

Policy Limits

The maximum amount an insurance policy will pay, regardless of how much our client is owed. Georgia minimum is 25/50 ($25K per person / $50K per accident). We always try to find out limits early.

MedPay

Medical payments coverage on our client's own policy. Pays medical bills up to the policy amount regardless of fault. Usually $2K–$10K. Always check if client has MedPay — it's money on the table.

How to Figure Out Which Insurance to Call

1
Get the police report — It identifies the at-fault driver and their insurance info
2
Client provides their own insurance info — For UM/UIM and MedPay claims
3
Send LORs to all applicable carriers — BI carrier, UM carrier (if applicable)
4
Open a claim with the at-fault driver's insurance ASAP

What is an LOR and Why We Send It

LOR — Letter of Representation

📄 An LOR formally notifies the insurance company: "We represent this person. All communication goes through our office — not through the client directly."

Once an LOR is received by insurance, they are legally required to communicate with our office, not our client. This protects the client from saying something that could hurt their case.

The 48-Hour Rule

⏰ LORs must be sent within 48 hours of signing the client. No exceptions. This is one of the most critical deadlines in the firm. Late LORs can allow insurance to contact and manipulate the client directly.

Which Template to Use

Template 01 — At-Fault (BI)
Template 02 — UM Carrier

Use Template 01 for the other driver's insurance (BI claim). Use Template 02 if the client has UM/UIM coverage on their own policy.

What an LOR Does

  • Notifies insurance that we represent the client
  • Directs all communication through our office
  • Stops insurance adjusters from contacting the client to get recorded statements or lowball offers
  • Starts the clock — insurance must now deal with us

What is an LOP and Why We Send It

LOP — Letter of Protection

🏥 An LOP tells a medical provider: "This patient has a personal injury case. We guarantee you will be paid from the settlement. Please treat them now, and we'll pay you when the case resolves."

Why This Matters

Many personal injury clients don't have health insurance — or their insurance won't cover accident-related treatment. Without an LOP, they might not be able to get the medical care they need to recover and to build their case.

The LOP bridges this gap. The provider agrees to wait for payment from the settlement proceeds instead of billing the client upfront.

⚠️ When a case settles, providers with LOPs get paid from the settlement before the client receives anything. These are called medical liens.

Template & Process

Template 12 — Letter of Protection
  • Sent to the treating provider (chiropractor, pain management clinic, etc.)
  • Provider signs and agrees to treat the client on lien
  • Provider holds their bill until settlement
  • At settlement, we negotiate the lien down when possible and pay from proceeds
Section 6 of 8
The Police Report

Why the Police Report Matters

📋 The police report is often the foundation of liability. It documents who was at fault, contains insurance information for all parties, and is one of the first documents we need after signing a client.

What It Contains

  • Names and contact info of all drivers involved
  • Insurance information for all parties
  • Officer's narrative of what happened
  • Who received citations (fault indicators)
  • Witness information
  • Photos and diagram of the scene (sometimes)

How to Request a Police Report

Template 65 — Standard Request
Template 66 — Atlanta PD
Template 67 — County Sheriff

The correct template depends on which jurisdiction responded to the accident. Your CM will guide you on which to use. Some reports can be obtained directly online (LexisNexis, local PD portals).

Why It Establishes Liability

Insurance companies look at the police report to determine whose insurance pays. If the at-fault driver received a citation, that's a strong liability indicator. If there's no police report, liability becomes harder to prove.

💡 Always try to get the police report within the first week of signing the client. It's a foundational document — everything else builds from it.

Medical Records and Bills

Why We Need Records and Bills

  • Medical records prove the client was injured — they show diagnoses, treatment notes, and documented pain
  • Medical bills show how much treatment cost — this is a core component of our demand to insurance
  • Without complete records and bills, we cannot send a demand. Without a demand, there's no settlement.
🚨 Incomplete records are one of the biggest delays in settlement. If a provider hasn't sent records, follow up every 14 days until received.

HIPAA Authorization

Template 44 — HIPAA Authorization

Before we can request any medical records, the client must sign a HIPAA Authorization form. This legally allows providers to release their private medical information to our office. Get this signed at intake.

Records Request Templates

Template 04 — Hospital Records
Template 05 — Chiro Records
Template 06 — Pain Mgmt Records
Template 07 — Primary Care

Use the template that matches the provider type. Your CM will confirm the correct one if you're unsure.

The Follow-Up Rule

📅 Follow up on outstanding records requests every 14 days until received. Log every follow-up in CasePeer. Providers are busy — you must stay on them.

What Happens When a Case Settles

The Settlement Process

When insurance agrees to pay, the settlement process begins. This is the finish line — but there are critical steps before the client gets their money.

1
Insurance agrees to pay a settlement amount
2
Attorney approves and client signs the ROS (Receipt of Settlement)
3
Insurance sends the check to our office (trust account)
4
Deductions are made from the settlement
5
Client receives their portion — net check distributed

Settlement Breakdown — Where the Money Goes

💰 Gross Settlement Amounte.g., $30,000
→ Attorney Fees (typically 33%)−$9,900
→ Case Expenses (copies, filing, etc.)−$500
→ Medical Bills / LOP Liens−$8,000
→ Health Insurance Liens (if any)−$0–varies
✅ Client's Net Recovery~$11,600

These numbers are examples only. Every case is different. The client always receives a detailed settlement breakdown before signing.

The ROS and the 14-Day Rule

The ROS (Receipt of Settlement) is the document the client signs to authorize distribution. Once the settlement check arrives at our office, the entire process — from check receipt to client payment — should take no more than 14 days.
