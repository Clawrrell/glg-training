Know Your Coverage Types

6 types, every case, no exceptions.
📋 Keisha Williams — Case Intake

You're opening Keisha's file. A delivery driver ran a red light and T-boned her driver's side door. Moderate injuries. You need to identify every possible coverage source before this case goes anywhere.

The at-fault driver works for a local pizza restaurant. Keisha has her own car insurance. Her husband also has a car. Let's map out the coverage landscape.

🛡️ Bodily Injury Liability

The at-fault driver's policy. Pays your client for their injuries. This is what most people think of when they say "the other driver's insurance." Minimum in GA: $25K/$50K.

🔰 UM / UIM

Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist. On the CLIENT's own policy. Steps in when at-fault driver has no insurance or not enough. Georgia requires it unless rejected in writing.

💊 MedPay

Medical Payments Coverage. No-fault — pays regardless of who caused the accident. Available on client's own policy. Covers medical bills up to the limit (typically $1K–$10K).

📄 PIP

Personal Injury Protection. Georgia is NOT a no-fault state — PIP is rare here. But some out-of-state policies include it. Always ask and check the dec page.

📚 Stacking

When multiple UM/UIM policies apply to the same claim. Household members' policies can stack. Multiple vehicles on one policy may stack. Can dramatically increase recovery.

🚛 Commercial Policies

If the at-fault driver was working — delivery, rideshare, trucking — their EMPLOYER's commercial policy may apply. Often much higher limits. Separate from the driver's personal policy.

How to Verify Coverage

Dec pages, calls, and databases — your three tools.
📋 Keisha Williams — Day 1 Investigation

You've mapped out the possible coverage sources. Now you need to confirm what's actually there. The at-fault driver gave you his insurance card. Keisha gave you hers. That's a starting point — not an answer.

📄 Tool #1: The Declarations Page (Dec Page)

The dec page is the summary sheet at the front of an insurance policy. It shows:
• Named insured(s) and vehicles covered
• Coverage types and limits (e.g., BI $100K/$300K, UM $100K, MedPay $5K)
• Policy period (is it active?)
• Premium paid (confirms coverage is in force)

Always request the full dec page — not just a summary from an adjuster. Adjusters sometimes describe coverage incorrectly or incompletely.

📞 Tool #2: The Claims Call

For the at-fault driver's carrier: Call the insurance company directly, report the claim, and ask for limits verbally. Always follow up in writing. Key questions:
• "What are the bodily injury liability limits?"
• "Is there an umbrella or commercial policy?"
• "Has the at-fault driver made any other claims this policy period?" (affects available limits)
• "Is coverage confirmed, or is there an investigation pending?"

🔍 Tool #3: The NAIC Database & State Insurance Commissioner

If you can't identify the at-fault driver's carrier, or if a carrier claims no policy exists, use:
• NAIC Consumer Insurance Search (naic.org) — look up licensed carriers in Georgia
• Georgia Office of Insurance & Safety Fire Commissioner — verify if a carrier is licensed, file complaints for non-disclosure
• ISO (Insurance Services Office) — used by attorneys to uncover umbrella and excess policies

⚠️ The insurance card is NOT enough. An insurance card only proves the policy existed at one point. You need the dec page to confirm it was active on the date of the accident AND to see actual coverage limits.
💡 Coverage denied? Under O.C.G.A. § 33-7-11(b)(1)(D)(iii), if the at-fault driver's insurer formally denies coverage — for any reason — that vehicle is treated as uninsured for UM purposes. Keisha's UM policy kicks in.

Limits vs. Liability — Not the Same Thing

Two different questions. Both matter. A lot.
📋 Keisha Williams — Two Questions About Coverage

Knowing the at-fault driver has insurance is just the start. You need to know two separate things: how much can they pay (limits), and can they be held responsible (liability)?

💰 Limits — How Much Can Be Paid

Policy limits are the maximum dollar amount an insurer will pay on a claim. They're stated on the dec page. A "$25,000/$50,000" policy means:
• Max $25,000 per injured person per accident
• Max $50,000 total for ALL injured people in one accident

Limits are fixed. You cannot make an insurer pay above their policy limits (unless there's a bad faith claim). Limits drive your strategy — if bills exceed limits, you look for additional coverage.

⚖️ Liability — Who Is Legally Responsible

Liability is the legal question: is the at-fault party actually responsible for causing the accident? Even if someone has high limits, an insurer can deny the claim by arguing their insured was NOT at fault.

Liability status determines:
• Whether the insurer will pay at all
• How quickly they'll engage in negotiations
• Whether you'll need to file suit

Until liability is accepted — either verbally or in writing — there is no settlement. You track liability status separately from limits status.

🚨 The Trap: High limits mean nothing if liability is disputed. A driver with $500,000 in coverage who claims the accident was your client's fault will not pay. Always track BOTH: limits confirmed ✓ / liability confirmed ✓ — separately.

UM/UIM — Claiming Against Your Own Client's Policy

Georgia law: the safety net when the at-fault driver can't pay enough.
📋 Keisha Williams — UM Claim Decision Point

The at-fault driver's insurer accepted liability but confirmed limits at $25,000. Keisha's bills are $47,000 and climbing. She's still in treatment. You need to open a UM claim on her own GEICO policy now — not later.

When UM/UIM Coverage Applies

  • At-fault driver has no insurance (truly uninsured)
  • At-fault driver's limits are less than total damages (underinsured)
  • At-fault driver's insurer formally denies coverage (treated as uninsured under O.C.G.A. § 33-7-11)
  • Hit-and-run where the driver is unknown — requires physical contact OR an independent eyewitness
  • At-fault driver cannot be located after diligent search

Excess vs. Reduced UM — Critical Distinction

Excess (Add-On) UM: The UM policy pays ON TOP of the at-fault driver's liability. Best for the client.
Example: $25K at-fault BI + $100K Excess UM = $125K total available.

Reduced (Offset) UM: The UM policy only pays the DIFFERENCE. The at-fault payout reduces the UM payment.
Example: $25K at-fault BI + $100K Reduced UM = UM pays only $75K (the offset), total still $100K from two sources combined.

⚡ Always identify Excess vs. Reduced. It changes the math on every case.

How to Open a UM Claim — Step by Step

  1. Confirm client has UM on their own policy (check dec page)
  2. Notify client's insurer in writing that a UM claim is being opened
  3. Provide: client's policy number, date of accident, description of accident, at-fault driver's information and insurer
  4. If at-fault driver's insurer offers policy limits — you MUST provide notice to client's UM insurer before settling with at-fault carrier (they have the right to consent)
  5. UM carrier assigns an adjuster and claim number — log both in CasePeer
  6. Obtain UM dec page to confirm Excess vs. Reduced and exact limits
🚨 Critical Trap: If the at-fault driver's insurer offers their policy limits ($25K) and you accept without first notifying Keisha's UM insurer, you may waive her right to the UM claim entirely. Always get UM insurer consent before settling the liability side.

Stacking & Commercial Vehicles

Finding every available dollar — including the big ones.
📋 Keisha Williams — Stacking Analysis

You've confirmed Keisha's UM ($100K Excess). Now you need to check whether her husband's policy stacks. And you need to nail down the restaurant's commercial policy — that could be the biggest number on this case.

📚 Stacking — The Priority Rules

Georgia allows UM stacking in many circumstances. When multiple policies are available, courts apply this priority order:

  1. 1st Priority: The insurer that received a premium from the injured person or a relative pays first
  2. 2nd Priority: The policy most closely associated with the injured person's situation
  3. 3rd Priority: The policy most related to the accident circumstances

Household relative stacking: Under O.C.G.A. § 33-7-11(b)(1)(B), spouses and relatives living in the same household are "insureds" under each other's UM policies — even if they weren't in each other's cars.

Anti-stacking clauses: Some policies have language limiting stacking. Always check policy language — and flag for the attorney if there's a dispute.

⚡ One rule: No double recovery. Keisha cannot collect more than her total proven damages, even if UM limits exceed them.

🚛 Commercial Vehicle Coverage — Different Rules

When the at-fault driver was working — delivery, rideshare, trucking, running work errands — the employer's commercial auto policy may apply. This is separate from and in addition to the driver's personal policy.

  • Commercial limits are typically much higher — $300K, $500K, $1M+
  • Rideshare (Uber/Lyft): Have their own commercial policies — $1M per incident when the driver has a passenger
  • Commercial trucks: Federal regulations (FMCSA) require minimum $750K for most trucks, $1M for hazmat
  • Even if the driver claims they were "off the clock," if they were performing any work-related task, the commercial policy may apply
  • Employer may also face liability for negligent hiring, supervision, or entrustment

Coverage + Case Value + CasePeer

How coverage shapes treatment planning — and where it all lives.
📋 Keisha Williams — Strategy Meeting

The attorney reviews the coverage picture you've built. $25K at-fault, $100K Excess UM (Keisha), $50K UM (husband), $500K commercial (restaurant). Potential total: $675K. Keisha's current bills: $47K. Treatment still ongoing.

The attorney says: "With $675K available, Keisha can complete every recommended treatment. Don't let her stop because of money anxiety. Coverage changes everything about how we guide this case."

💡 Coverage → Treatment Planning Connection

Available coverage directly affects what treatment a client can realistically pursue:

  • High coverage available: Client can complete full recommended treatment without worrying about billing caps — leads to stronger case and better outcome
  • Low coverage / policy limits case: Treatment planning may need to be adjusted — "bill to cap" scenarios require attorney guidance
  • No coverage identified yet: Client may self-limit treatment — your job is to identify coverage quickly so this doesn't happen
  • MedPay / PIP: Can be used to pay bills while the main case is pending — reduces client financial stress during treatment

See also: Case Timeline & Treatment Progression — treatment gaps, missed appointments, and premature discharge all damage case value. Knowing coverage exists removes one of the main reasons clients stop treatment early.

📱 CasePeer Documentation — Coverage Verification Fields

Every coverage item must be documented in CasePeer under the case's Insurance/Coverage section:

  • At-fault carrier: Carrier name, claim number, adjuster name + phone, BI limits, liability status
  • Client's own coverage: Carrier, policy number, UM type (Excess/Reduced), UM limits, MedPay limits, claim number if opened
  • Household UM: Each policy, carrier, limits, relationship to client
  • Commercial policy: Employer name, carrier, claim number, limits, adjuster
  • Status flags: "Limits confirmed ✓", "Liability accepted ✓", "UM claim opened ✓", "Dec page on file ✓"

⚡ If it's not in CasePeer, it doesn't exist. Verbal knowledge in your head helps nobody when you're out sick.
