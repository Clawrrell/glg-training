Section 1

The 14-Day Rule — Why It Matters

Previously you learned: In NH-9, you explored the fundamentals of client communication — why it matters, how clients experience the firm, and the basics of proactive outreach. Now we go deeper: the specific protocol, scripts, documentation standards, and conversation techniques that make communication work at GLG.

The Core Principle

Communication IS the product. Clients do not experience the legal file — they experience every phone call, every update, every moment of silence between contacts. Most client complaints are not about case outcomes. They are about feeling ignored. The 14-day protocol exists to eliminate that.

Required Contact Frequency

TriggerStandard
Pre-Lit active casesOne proactive update every 14 days — no exceptions
Settlement offersPhone call to client within 24 hours of receipt
Client calls/messagesResponse within 24 hours — non-negotiable
Litigation casesMinimum every 30 days

The Standard Is Not Optional

Updates must occur even if there is no substantive case change. The client needs to hear from you. If a client calls asking for an update, we already failed the standard.

One Behavior, Three Systems' Payoff

The 14-day client call is simultaneously:

1
A Satisfaction Tool
Keeps the client informed and feeling cared for. They don't wonder "Is anyone working on my case?" because you just told them.
2
A Referral Generator
Satisfied clients tell their family and friends. The story they tell — "they called me every two weeks just to check in" — is the referral engine. Not the billboard.
3
A Case-Value Driver
Treatment compliance = bigger medical file = stronger demand = bigger settlement. The check-in call catches gaps before they become case-killers.

The Business Case

  • Proactive updates eliminate 80% of complaint calls
  • 4 of the 5 biggest drivers of client disloyalty are effort-related — clients who have to call multiple times, repeat themselves, or chase updates leave and never refer
  • Every unreturned call is a lost referral
  • Communication compliance is audited monthly and tied to bonus eligibility

One call. Every fourteen days. Three systems' payoff. Non-negotiable.

Section 2

What Every Update Must Include

The Five Required Elements

Every client update — regardless of case stage — must address all five of these elements. Miss one and the client leaves the call with unanswered questions.

1
Current Case Stage
Where the case is right now, using CasePeer status names translated into plain language. "We're in the treatment phase" — not "Status: Pre-Lit Active."
2
What We Are Waiting On
Treatment completion, medical records, insurer response, lien resolution — name the specific item that is in progress or pending.
3
Next Step
Specifically what happens next and who is doing it. "I'm going to request your MRI records this week" — not "We'll be in touch."
4
Realistic Timeframe
A date or range the client can hold onto. "We expect to hear back from the adjuster within the next two to three weeks." Never say "soon."
5
Expectation Reset (If Needed)
Especially important after delays or unexpected developments. Re-anchor the client to a realistic outlook so they aren't waiting for something that won't happen.

Mandatory Expectation-Setting Language

Every update during treatment and records phase must include language like: "Personal injury cases often take several months while treatment and records are completed. Your case is progressing as expected, and we will update you again by [date]."

Never Overpromise

Do not overpromise timelines, settlement values, or case outcomes. Never quote a number. Never give a range. Overpromising creates complaints. Accurate expectations create trust.

Section 3

Documentation Standards

The Rule

If it's not in CasePeer, it didn't happen. Every client contact must be logged the same day it occurs. Not after lunch. Not at the end of the day. Before the next task.

Required Note Format

DATE | METHOD | WHO | WHAT | NEXT STEPS

  • Date: Date of communication
  • Method: Phone / Text / Email / Letter / In-Person
  • Contact: Full name and role of person you spoke with
  • Summary: What was discussed — be specific
  • Next Steps: What happens next, with deadlines

Bad Note vs. Good Note

Bad: "Called client about case. Left voicemail."
No date. No phone number. No detail about the call's purpose. No next steps.

Good: "5/8/2026 — Phone call (outbound) to client Marcus Johnson at (404) 555-7890. Called to follow up on treatment status — last chiro visit was 4/15. No answer. Left voicemail: reminded client to continue treatment. Sent follow-up text immediately after. Next: if no response by 5/12, call again."

The Handoff Test

Could a colleague who has never touched this file read your note and pick up where you left off — without calling you? If yes, it's a good note. If no, rewrite it. Every note is a handoff package for whoever picks up this case next.

Attempted Contacts Count

Attempted contacts must also be logged — note the attempt, method, and result (no answer, voicemail left, etc.). Missing documentation will be treated as if the contact did not occur during audits.

The Follow-Up Text Rule

After EVERY call attempt — answered or not — send a follow-up text to the client. Texts create a written record, show the client you're actively working their case, and give them a way to respond on their own time.

Log What They Said AND How They Felt

If a client tells you they are frustrated, scared, confused, or relieved — log that. Not just the case facts. A case manager who records facts but ignores the person leaves a record that looks complete but misses what matters most. Clients who feel heard trust the firm.

The Never-Miss-Twice Rule: One skipped entry is a mistake. Two in a row is a habit forming in the wrong direction. If you find a gap, log retroactively with today's date — note it as retroactive and explain why it was missed. Never backdate entries.
Section 4

Treatment Management

Why Treatment Drives Case Value

Treatment compliance is the ceiling of what the negotiation can recover. A complete medical record — consistent appointments, documented pain levels, noted improvements and setbacks — gives the team everything needed to build the strongest possible demand. Gaps in treatment give the other side a documented reason to reduce it.

The Treatment Check-In Call

Every 14 days during active treatment. This is how you protect the case value and maintain the client relationship. The call follows a specific structure:

1
Open With Care — Person Before File
"How are you doing? How are you feeling?" — not "Are you going to your appointments?" The client who feels the firm sees them as a person is more engaged and more likely to refer.
2
Ask About Treatment Progress
"Are you still going to all of your appointments?" If they report pain — acknowledge it first. "I'm sorry you're still dealing with that. Please make sure you're telling your doctor exactly how you feel."
3
Check for Gaps or Issues
"Have you missed any appointments recently, or had any trouble getting to your provider?" If gaps exist — find the real barrier before explaining consequences. Ask: "What's the real challenge here?"
4
Share Case Status
Even when nothing has changed: "Nothing has changed yet, and at this stage that is completely normal. We have not forgotten about you — that's why I'm calling."
5
Close With a Commitment
Get a specific appointment date. "Your next appointment with [Provider] — do you know when that is?" A commitment with a date in CasePeer is real. "I'll try to go" is not a commitment.

Treatment Gap Call — When Gaps Appear

Use when a client has not treated in 2+ weeks with no explanation. Open as an ally, not an auditor. Find the real barrier — transportation, billing confusion, scheduling, "I feel better." Fix the actual problem.

Common Barriers and Solutions

BarrierSolution
TransportationIdentify rideshare options, ask if family can help, check if provider offers flexibility
Billing confusionClarify: treatment costs are handled through the case under a Letter of Protection. Contact the provider to resolve.
SchedulingOffer to contact provider directly to reschedule. Ask what times work.
"I feel better"Acknowledge the progress, then explain why full documentation protects the final result. Escalate to attorney.

Escalation Triggers

Gap of 30+ days: Escalate to Harrell or Adriauna immediately.
Client wants to stop treating: Do NOT accept without attorney sign-off. Ever.
3 unanswered attempts in 2 weeks: Escalate — do not let the file sit idle.

Section 5

Difficult Conversations

The Difference Between a Crisis and an Opportunity

The difference between a difficult conversation that destroys the relationship and one that strengthens it is almost always preparation and sequence. Prepare your approach, then make the call.

Pre-Call Preparation (Do This Every Time)

  • Know the file: Pull CasePeer. Know the current stage, last contact, any open promises, any commitments unfulfilled.
  • Run the accusation audit: List every legitimate concern the client could raise. Decide which ones to address proactively. Pre-empting complaints removes their sting.
  • Set your register: Slow down. Lower your voice slightly. The tone in the first 30 seconds determines what's possible.
  • Name your goal: "I want this client to feel heard and leave with a concrete next step." That's a goal. "I want to avoid getting yelled at" is avoidance.

Safety Before Content

People can only absorb information when they feel safe. When a client is emotionally activated, their mind is managing a threat — not processing your update. Your first job is to restore safety — make them feel you are on their side, not the adversary. Until safety is established, nothing you say will land.

Name the Emotion Before Addressing Content

Naming what someone is feeling is the fastest way to defuse it. This is not the same as saying "I understand." Describe what you observe:

  • "It sounds like you're frustrated that no one has reached out."
  • "It seems like you feel like you've been left in the dark."
  • "It looks like this isn't what you were expecting."

Then stop talking. Let the statement land. The client will confirm it, correct it, or expand on it. All three outcomes move the conversation forward.

The STATE Method for High-Stakes Conversations

When a conversation turns high-stakes — client threatening to fire the firm, post-settlement disputes, missed milestones:

S
Share Facts First
"Here's what the records show..." — lead with objective facts, not opinions or accusations.
T
Tell Your Story Tentatively
"My read is that we are waiting on X..." — share your interpretation as a possibility, not a certainty.
A
Ask for Their View
"Help me understand what you're seeing on your end." — invite their perspective before defending yours.
T
Talk Tentatively
"Could it be that..." rather than "Obviously this is..." — frame statements as questions, not verdicts.
E
Encourage Testing
Open dialogue, don't shut it down. Make it safe for the client to disagree or push back.

The Silence/Violence Trap

Don't go quiet (avoiding the conversation) or attack (defending aggressively). Both destroy trust. When a conversation turns unsafe, step out of content and address safety: "I want to make sure we resolve this — can I share what I'm seeing on our end?"

When to Escalate

  • Client has complained 2 or more times without resolution
  • Client expresses intent to terminate representation
  • Client threatens to contact the bar
  • A settlement dispute cannot be resolved at the paralegal level
  • An attorney callback has been requested and not fulfilled within 24 hours
Section 6

Call Script Mastery

Three Scripts, One Goal

Every outbound client call at GLG follows a tested structure. You don't need to recite scripts word for word — but you do need to know the structure and hit every required element. Here are the three scripts you'll use most.

1. Treatment Check-In Call

When: Every 14 days during active treatment

  • Open: "How are you doing?" — person first, file second
  • Ask about treatment: "Are you feeling any progress with your injuries?"
  • Check compliance: "Have you missed any appointments?"
  • Share status: Even if nothing changed — "Silence right now means the process is running exactly as it should."
  • Close with commitment: Get the next appointment date. "Your next appointment is when?" Write it down.
  • "And what else?" Ask at least once before closing. It surfaces hidden concerns.

2. Treatment Gap Call

When: Client hasn't treated in 2+ weeks

  • Open as ally: "I'm not calling to lecture you. I'm calling because I'm on your side."
  • Find the barrier: "What's been going on since your last appointment?" Then: "And what else?" (at least twice)
  • Acknowledge what they're feeling: Reflect their words back. "It sounds like [what you heard]. Is that right?"
  • Name what's at stake (AFTER listening): "Insurance adjusters look for gaps. Every appointment you kept is evidence working for you."
  • Earn the commitment: Specific date, specific provider. Confirm three times in different words.

3. Callback / Follow-Up Call

When: Returning a missed call or following up on a prior conversation

  • Open: "I'm returning your call from earlier today. I'm sorry I missed you."
  • Kickstart: "What's on your mind?" — let them set the direction
  • Handle the issue: If you can answer, answer. If not: "I want to give you an accurate answer. Let me look into that and get back to you by [specific time]."
  • Close: "Is there anything else I can help you with?" Then "And what else?"

The "And What Else?" Technique

Always ask "And what else?" at least once before closing any call. It consistently surfaces concerns the client didn't raise on their own — financial stress, anxiety about timing, confusion about next steps. Takes 15 seconds to catch here, much longer to resolve later.

Commitment Capture — The Rule of Three

Every call that ends well should end with a specific, documented commitment. Confirm it three times in different words. This is the difference between a genuine agreement and a polite "yes" to end the call:

  • The plan: "So you're going back to [Provider] by [date] — that's our plan?"
  • The understanding: "And you understand why it matters — getting back quickly is the most important thing for your case right now?"
  • The follow-up: "I'm going to check back on [date] to make sure everything is on track. Does that work?"
Under-promise, then over-deliver. Resist the urge to give an optimistic number to reduce anxiety. Quote conservatively. Promise less than you believe you will deliver. Then beat the number. The moment a client receives more than they were promised is the story they tell their family.
Section 7

Scenarios — Practice Rounds

Apply What You've Learned

Walk through these realistic PI case situations. For each, think about what you'd say and do — then read the correct approach.

Scenario A

Marcus Williams signed retainer 16 days ago. CasePeer shows no contact since intake. What do you do?

Correct Approach

  • You're already 2 days past the 14-day standard. This is urgent.
  • Call Marcus immediately. Prepare an update with all 5 elements: current stage, what you're waiting on, next step, timeframe, and expectation-setting language.
  • After the call (answered or not), send a follow-up text and log everything in CasePeer same day.
  • Set a CasePeer task for the next 14-day contact. Never rely on memory.
Scenario B

Dana Rivera calls confused. She received an $850 bill from her chiropractor. She thinks she has to pay out of pocket and is considering stopping treatment.

Correct Approach

  • First: Acknowledge what she's feeling. "I can see why that bill would be stressful."
  • Explain the Letter of Protection: "You should not be paying anything out of pocket. Your treatment costs are handled through the case."
  • "Do not pay that bill. I will contact the provider directly today and get this corrected."
  • Get her commitment to continue treatment. Lock in a specific appointment date.
  • Log the call in CasePeer: include her concern, what you explained, what you promised, and the deadline.
  • Contact the provider today — and document that follow-up too.
Scenario C

James Thompson hasn't been to physical therapy in 3 weeks. He says "I'm feeling a lot better. I don't see the point in going anymore."

Correct Approach

  • First: Acknowledge his progress genuinely. "I'm really glad you're feeling better."
  • Ask "And what else?" — dig deeper. Is there another reason? Transportation? Cost? Frustration?
  • Be direct about the risk: "If you stop before your doctor officially releases you, the insurance company will argue your injuries resolved on their own. That argument lowers what we can recover."
  • Offer to have the attorney call to explain what's at stake for his specific case.
  • Never accept a client stopping treatment without attorney approval.
  • Escalate to Harrell or Adriauna immediately.

Every call is a chance to protect the case, protect the client, and earn the next referral.
