Section 1

Why Medical Records Drive Case Value

Medical records aren't just paperwork — they're the foundation of every personal injury case. No records? No case. Weak records? Small settlement. Strong, complete records? Maximum value.

📋 What Medical Records Prove

  • Causation — the injury was caused by the accident (not pre-existing)
  • Severity — how serious the injuries are (diagnosis, imaging findings)
  • Treatment — what was done, by whom, for how long
  • Prognosis — what the future looks like (ongoing pain, permanent injury)
  • Economic damages — the actual dollar cost of treatment (from medical bills)
💰 Records = Money
  • Settlement multiplier is based on total medical bills
  • More documented treatment = higher demand
  • Missing records = missing dollars
  • Defense uses gaps to argue exaggeration
⚠️ When Records Are Missing
  • Insurer questions whether injury happened
  • We can't prove the full extent of harm
  • Liens may surface at settlement we didn't know about
  • Case could get stuck at demand stage

⚠️ The Golden Rule of Records

Always request both medical records AND itemized billing in the same letter. Records show the injury. Bills show the money. You need both to build a demand. No exceptions.

Think of medical records as the evidence file for the case.

The better the documentation, the stronger the case. Your job is to make sure nothing falls through the cracks.

Section 2

Types of Medical Records in PI Cases

Different providers create different types of records. You need to know what each one contains and why it matters.

🚑
Emergency Room (ER) Records
First treatment after the accident. Critical because they document the initial presentation — complaints at the scene, mechanism of injury, and early diagnosis. Why it matters: Insurers look at ER records to see what the client complained about immediately after the crash. Complaints consistent with later treatment = strong causation link. Request these FIRST, as soon as the case opens.
🔬
MRI & Imaging Reports
X-rays, MRIs, CT scans — objective proof of injury. A herniated disc on an MRI is worth far more than just complaints of back pain. Why it matters: Objective findings (things the doctor can see, not just what the patient says) drive up settlement value significantly. Soft tissue injuries with no imaging = lower value. Disc herniations, fractures, and tears = high value. Always request the radiology report AND the images themselves.
🦴
Orthopedic Notes
Specialist notes from bone and joint doctors. Often involved in fractures, joint injuries, surgical cases. Why it matters: Orthopedic treatment signals more serious injury. Surgical recommendations dramatically increase case value. Look for: diagnosis, referrals, surgical recommendations, functional limitations.
🏥
Physical Therapy (PT) Notes
Session-by-session notes from the physical therapist. Why it matters: PT notes document consistency of treatment and improvement (or lack thereof). Frequent sessions = active treatment = engaged client. Look for: frequency, number of sessions, therapist's functional notes, discharge status.
💊
Pain Management Notes
Notes from pain management doctors — often involve injections (epidurals, nerve blocks) or prescription management. Why it matters: Pain management treatment signals chronic or severe pain that didn't resolve with basic care. Injections are expensive and indicate serious ongoing pain. High value for demand purposes.
🧠
Primary Care & Follow-Up Notes
Family doctor or urgent care visits after the accident. Why it matters: These often bridge the gap between ER and specialist care. They document ongoing complaints and referrals. If a client went to their PCP complaining of neck pain two weeks post-accident, that's important.

✅ Build the Full Provider List at Intake

During intake, ask the client: Where did you go after the accident? ER? Urgent care? What doctors are you seeing now? Build a complete list and send records requests to every single one — immediately.

Section 3

Reading Records & Medical Bills

You don't need to be a doctor — but you do need to know what you're looking at when records arrive. Here's what to focus on.

🔍 What to Look for in Medical Records

  • ICD-10 Diagnosis Codes — the standardized code for each diagnosis (see below)
  • Treatment dates — first visit, last visit, any gaps in between
  • Chief complaints — what the patient reported (neck pain, headaches, etc.)
  • Doctor's findings and opinions — "patient presents with..." and "in my opinion..."
  • Causation language — "consistent with motor vehicle accident" is gold
  • Referrals — being referred to a specialist = more serious injury
  • MMI notation — "Maximum Medical Improvement" = treatment is done

📊 ICD-10 Codes — The Basics

ICD-10 codes are alphanumeric codes that classify every diagnosis. You don't need to memorize them, but you should recognize common ones in PI cases:

CodeWhat It MeansValue Signal
S13.4Cervical sprain/strain (whiplash)Moderate
M54.5Low back painModerate
M51.1Disc herniation with radiculopathyHigh
S72.0Femur (hip/thigh) fractureHigh
S06.0Concussion / traumatic brain injuryHigh
S43.0Shoulder dislocationHigh
M79.3Panniculitis / soft tissue injuryLower

💵 How to Read a Medical Bill

Medical bills use CPT codes (Current Procedural Terminology) to identify each service billed:

TermWhat It Means
CPT CodeThe procedure code — e.g., 99213 = office visit (moderate complexity)
Facility chargeWhat the hospital/facility bills (usually higher)
Professional chargeWhat the doctor personally bills (separate from facility)
Billed amountThe full "list price" — often inflated. This is what we use in the demand.
Adjusted/paid amountWhat insurance actually paid (much lower). We focus on the billed amount.
Balance dueWhat's still owed — often zero if insurance paid, or the full amount if LOP

Important: For demand purposes, we use the billed amount (also called "charges"), not what insurance actually paid. A $10,000 ER bill is a $10,000 ER bill, even if the insurer negotiated it down to $3,000.

⚠️ Facility vs. Professional Charges

When a client is treated at a hospital or surgery center, you'll often receive TWO separate bills: one from the facility (the hospital) and one from the provider (the doctor or surgeon). Make sure you collect BOTH. They often come from different billing departments and are easy to miss.

Section 4

Treatment Gaps & the Records Timeline

One of the most critical skills in this job is spotting treatment gaps — and understanding why they can seriously hurt a case.

❌ What Is a Treatment Gap?

A treatment gap is any period of 30 days or more where the client stopped treating with NO documented medical reason. The insurer's position: "If you were really hurt, you would have kept going to the doctor."

📉 How Gaps Hurt the Case

  • The insurer will reduce the settlement offer, arguing the client "recovered"
  • The attorney's ability to argue ongoing pain is weakened without consistent records
  • The gap becomes ammunition for the defense: "Your client felt fine — look, they stopped treating"
  • A gap near the end of treatment is especially damaging at demand time

✅ What You Can Do

When reviewing incoming records, map out the treatment timeline. If you notice a gap, flag it to the Case Manager immediately. The CM may need to reach out to the client to understand why (life circumstances, transportation, etc.) — and get it documented.

📅 The Records Timeline — What to Expect

Records don't arrive instantly. Here's the general timeline to know:

1
Day 1 (Case Opens): Send Records Requests
Send the request with HIPAA auth to every known provider. Start the 14-day follow-up clock.
2
Days 14–30: First Follow-Up
If no records received, call the provider. Confirm they received your request and HIPAA auth. Ask for ETA. Log the call.
3
30–90 Days: Active Treatment Period
Client is still treating. Records will be "interim" — not final. Keep following up every 14 days. You'll likely get records in batches.
4
Treatment End: Final Records Request
When client finishes treatment, send a final records request for all records THROUGH the date of last visit. Make sure you have everything, not just what you received earlier.
5
All Records In: Ready for Demand
Once you have records AND billing from every provider, the CM can build the demand package. No missing records = no demand. Period.

⚠️ Records Arrive AFTER Treatment Ends

For ongoing treatment, you can only get records for visits that have already happened. You cannot get "future" records. This means you'll often get records in waves — some early, some much later. Always do a final, comprehensive records pull after the client is done treating. Don't assume what you received mid-treatment was everything.

Section 5

Red Flags to Spot in Medical Records

When records come in, you're not just filing them — you're scanning for problems. Here's what to watch for and flag to the Case Manager immediately:

🚩
Pre-Existing Injuries to the Same Body Part
If records show the client already had a bad back before the accident, the insurer will argue we're claiming pre-existing damage. The attorney needs to know this early so they can address it in the demand (aggravation of prior condition is still compensable — but it needs to be handled carefully).
🚩
Inconsistency Between ER Complaints and Later Treatment
Client complained of only wrist pain at the ER, but is now treating for neck and back. The insurer will question whether the neck/back injury was from the accident. Flag this for the CM — there may be a medical explanation, but it needs to be addressed.
🚩
Client Missed Appointments
Records often note "patient no-showed" or "patient cancelled." Multiple missed appointments creates the treatment gap problem AND suggests the client isn't compliant. Flag to the CM so they can reach out to the client.
🚩
A Provider You Didn't Know About
A record from Provider A mentions that the client was also seeing Provider B — but you never sent a records request to Provider B. This means missing records and possibly a missing lien. Add Provider B to your list immediately.
🚩
Discharge Against Medical Advice (AMA)
If a client left the ER or hospital against medical advice, the insurer will use this to argue they weren't following doctor's orders. Flag to CM.
🚩
Records Mention Alcohol or Drug Use at Time of Accident
This is a significant legal issue. Flag to the attorney immediately — do not handle this on your own. This affects liability and damages.

You are the first set of eyes on these records.

You don't have to know how to fix every red flag — but you do need to flag them to the CM immediately. Don't file something that looks concerning and say nothing. Speak up. That's part of your job.
