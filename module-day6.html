Section 2

What Records Matter Most for Case Value

GLG Evidence Hierarchy

Not all medical records are equal. Objective findings (MRI, X-ray, EMG) carry more weight than subjective complaints. Specialist reports matter more than urgent care visits. Emergency room records immediately after the accident are crucial for causation.

🚨
Emergency Room Records — Highest Priority
ER records from the day of the accident establish causation and document the initial injury complaints. These are often the most important records for proving the accident caused the injuries.
🔬
Diagnostic Imaging — Objective Evidence
X-rays, MRIs, CT scans provide objective proof of injuries that can't be disputed. Normal imaging doesn't mean no injury, but abnormal findings strongly support the case.
👨‍⚕️
Specialist Reports — Expert Opinions
Orthopedic surgeons, neurologists, pain management doctors carry more weight than general practitioners. Their diagnosis and treatment recommendations justify higher case values.
⚡
Diagnostic Tests — Functional Evidence
EMG (nerve conduction), bone scans, discograms show functional damage that supports ongoing pain complaints and permanent injury claims.
💉
Injection Procedures — Treatment Intensity
Epidural steroid injections, trigger point injections, and other pain procedures document severe, ongoing symptoms that justify higher settlements.

💰 Case Value Indicators to Flag

  • Surgery recommended or performed — Immediately increases case value significantly
  • Permanent restrictions — Work limitations, weight restrictions, activity limitations
  • Chronic pain management — Long-term pain clinic treatment indicates permanent injury
  • Cognitive issues — Concussion symptoms, memory problems, concentration difficulties
  • Multiple body systems affected — Neck + back + headaches = more complex injury
  • Failed conservative treatment — When PT, chiropractic, medication don't resolve symptoms

✅ Building the Medical Narrative

Strong cases tell a clear medical story: client was healthy → accident occurred → immediate symptoms developed → treatment was necessary and appropriate → injuries caused ongoing problems → future treatment may be needed. Gaps or inconsistencies in this narrative weaken case value.

🚨 Case Value Killers to Watch For

Pre-existing conditions with similar symptoms, large treatment gaps, missed appointments, discharged for non-compliance, providers noting malingering or exaggeration, client working full-time while claiming disability, or inconsistent symptom complaints across providers.

Lawyer Negotiation: Evidence Preparation

Medical record analysis prepares for settlement negotiation. Identify the strongest medical evidence first, understand potential weaknesses, and organize the medical story in a way that supports maximum case value. The adjuster will scrutinize the same records — be prepared.

Section 3

How Medical Evidence Supports Safety Rule Violations

Medical Evidence Strategy

Medical records don't just show injuries — they show the danger to the community. When medical evidence demonstrates that the defendant's conduct could have seriously injured anyone, it activates the jury's protective instincts and justifies full compensation.

⚖️ From Medical Findings to Safety Rules

  • Mechanism of injury — How the crash force moved through the client's body applies to anyone in that situation
  • Injury severity — If this client suffered herniated discs, anyone could have suffered the same or worse
  • Treatment necessity — The medical care required shows the real consequences of the defendant's violation
  • Recovery timeline — How long healing took demonstrates the true impact on a person's life
  • Ongoing effects — Permanent restrictions show lifelong consequences of momentary negligence

🛡️ PI strategy Medical Analysis Framework

Step 1: Identify the Safety Rule

What specific safety rule did the defendant violate? "A driver must maintain a safe following distance to prevent rear-end collisions." The medical records show what happens when this rule is broken.

Step 2: Document the Mechanism of Harm

Use medical records to explain exactly how the safety rule violation caused injury. "The impact forced the client's head forward and back, stretching ligaments beyond their normal range, causing permanent damage."

Step 3: Show Community Risk

Frame the injury as something that could happen to anyone. "These same forces could have injured any occupant — a child, an elderly person, a pregnant woman — with potentially catastrophic results."

Step 4: Demonstrate Real Consequences

Medical records show the actual cost of rule-breaking. "The medical evidence shows this violation required 6 months of treatment, multiple specialists, and ongoing pain management."

Step 5: Establish Need for Enforcement

Full compensation enforces the safety rule for everyone's benefit. "Holding defendants accountable for the full medical consequences protects the entire community from similar violations."

✅ Medical Records That Support PI strategy Arguments

ER records documenting immediate pain, imaging showing structural damage, specialist reports recommending ongoing care, and pain management records showing chronic effects all support the argument that safety rule violations have serious, lasting consequences for real people.

⚠️ PI strategy Application in Demand Letters

When preparing demand letters, frame medical evidence in terms of community safety and rule enforcement. Don't just list injuries — explain how these injuries demonstrate why the safety rule exists and why full compensation serves everyone's interest in safe driving.

Medical records tell two stories: what happened to this client, and what could happen to anyone.

Section 5

Explaining Medical Findings to Clients in Plain Language

Client Communication: Clarity Over Complexity

Clients need to understand their own medical situation to make informed decisions. Your job is to translate medical jargon into plain English that helps clients understand what happened, what treatment is needed, and what the long-term outlook might be.

🗣️ Translation Techniques

  • Use everyday analogies — "The disc is like a jelly donut. When it herniates, the jelly squeezes out and presses on nerves."
  • Explain cause and effect — "The accident forced your neck beyond its normal range, stretching ligaments that hold vertebrae in place."
  • Describe location specifically — "C5-C6 means the 5th and 6th vertebrae in your neck, right where it curves."
  • Connect symptoms to findings — "The MRI shows disc herniation at L4-L5, which explains the pain going down your left leg."
  • Discuss treatment rationale — "The doctor wants to try steroid injections to reduce swelling around the nerve."
📋
Prepare Before the Call
Review the medical records, understand the diagnosis, and prepare plain-language explanations. Don't try to explain medical findings on the fly during client calls.
🎯
Start with the Big Picture
"The MRI shows the accident caused two problems in your lower back. Let me explain what we found and what it means for your treatment."
💬
Check for Understanding
"Does that make sense so far?" "Do you have questions about what the doctor found?" Don't assume they understand — confirm it.
📝
Document the Conversation
Log in CasePeer what you explained and what questions the client asked. This helps with future calls and shows client was informed about their medical situation.

Radical Candor: Care Personally + Challenge Directly

Be honest about medical findings, both good and bad. If imaging is normal, don't oversell the findings. If there are concerning results, don't minimize them. Clients need accurate information to make informed decisions about treatment and settlement.

💬 Sample Client Explanations

"Herniated Disc" Translation

"The MRI shows one of the cushions between your vertebrae — we call it a disc — got damaged in the accident. Part of the disc material is now pressing on a nerve, which is why you feel pain shooting down your leg."

"Cervical Strain" Translation

"The muscles and ligaments in your neck got stretched beyond their normal limits during the crash. Think of it like pulling a rubber band too far — it gets injured and takes time to heal properly."

"Chronic Pain" Translation

"The doctor is saying your pain has lasted longer than the typical healing time. This suggests the accident caused changes that may need ongoing management rather than just time to heal."

"Conservative Treatment" Translation

"Before considering surgery, the doctor wants to try non-surgical treatments first — physical therapy, medication, maybe injections. Many people improve with these approaches."

✅ Building Client Confidence

When clients understand their medical situation, they feel more in control and make better decisions about treatment. Clear explanations also prepare them for deposition questions about their injuries and treatment.

🚨 What Not to Do

Never diagnose, predict outcomes, or give medical advice. Explain what the records show, not what it means for their future. "The doctor will discuss treatment options" not "You'll probably need surgery." Stay in your lane — legal assistance, not medical guidance.
